Growing Innovation Capability in Teams

Kia ora, welcome to my PhD artefact. This explores growing innovation capability in teams. It has five layers.

First, Hautū Waka — a wayfinding narrative developed by Rereata Makiha, Roimata Taniwha-Pao'o and Ayla Hoeta. It holds the team and the work, helping people navigate when the landscape shifts beneath them.

Second, tools — adapted from existing practice, grounding each stage in concrete action.

Third, muscles — collective dispositions that emerge through use, enabling teams to move through uncertain, complex work. Framed through Te Whare Tapa Whā.

Fourth, the mycorrhizal network — the reading, watching and listening that fed this work. The underground connections that made this vision possible.

Fifth, the tap root — my positionality. Why I care, where I stand, how I see. The ground everything else grows from.

Hautū Waka is a Māori wayfinding framework developed at Auckland Co-design Lab by Roimata Taniwha-Pao'o and Ayla Hoeta, with guidance from Matua Rereata Makiha. It's designed for situations where you can't know the path before walking it.

I chose it for three reasons. First, it assumes complexity — you read signs, make choices, possibly return. The waka can go forward, around, or back. Second, the framework is recognised rather than taught. For Māori and Pacific peoples, wayfinding is already familiar; for others, journeying is universal enough to carry meaning without requiring methodology training. Third, as Tangata Tiriti, I wanted to work with knowledge from this place, Aotearoa New Zealand. There is much wisdom in these lands, waters, and skies, and I am grateful for the perspective I have been allowed access to.
