This PhD explores how innovation capability emerges through relationships rather than being built in individuals. The research works with teams navigating complexity — conditions where what worked last month stops working, where the destination shifts mid-journey.

Hautū Waka is a Māori wayfinding framework developed at Auckland Co-design Lab by Roimata Taniwha-Pao'o and Ayla Hoeta, with guidance from Matua Rereata Makiha. It's designed for situations where you can't know the path before walking it.

I chose it for three reasons. First, it assumes complexity — you read signs, make choices, possibly return. The waka can go forward, around, or back. Second, the framework is recognised rather than taught. For Māori and Pacific peoples, wayfinding is already familiar; for others, journeying is universal enough to carry meaning without requiring methodology training. Third, as Tangata Tiriti, I wanted to work with knowledge from this place.
